The relapse rate is down. The MRI looks stable. By every clinical measure, the treatment is "working." And yet people living with MS keep losing movement — the grip that used to hold a mug, the leg that drags by afternoon, the balance that shifts whether the chart says it should or not. 1. Your Medication Was Designed to Do One Thing — And Movement Recovery Isn't It

🛡️ The Treatment Gap

Disease-modifying therapies do something genuinely important: they slow the immune system's attack on myelin. For many people, they reduce relapse frequency and slow the rate of new lesion formation. That's real.

But not a single FDA-approved MS drug is designed to repair the myelin that's already been damaged. Not Ocrevus. Not Kesimpta. Not Tecfidera. They defend the wiring. They don't rebuild it.

This is why an MRI can look stable while someone's walking gets worse. The immune attack has slowed. The underlying repair process hasn't started. And the movement keeps slipping in the gap between those two things.

2. The Reason Movement Keeps Declining Isn't the Disease "Progressing" — It's a Supply Problem

🔬 The Remyelination Gap

MS strips the protective myelin coating off nerve fibres. Without it, movement signals from the brain travel through damaged wiring — arriving late, distorted, or not at all. That's the leg that drags. The hand that won't grip. The foot that doesn't lift.

The brain has cells called oligodendrocytes whose entire job is to repair and replace that myelin. They're ready to work. But they need specific raw materials to do it — particularly a protein called Nerve Growth Factor (NGF) that activates the remyelination process.

Without NGF, the repair crew shows up but has nothing to build with. No amount of medication changes this. The supply shortage is separate from the immune suppression — addressing one doesn't address the other.

3. Chronic Neuroinflammation Keeps Blocking the Repair Even When Relapses Are Under Control

🔥 The Inflammation That Stays

Here's what most treatment conversations leave out: even when the acute autoimmune attack on myelin is slowed by a DMT, chronic low-level neuroinflammation continues in the affected areas of the nervous system. Overactive microglia — the brain's immune cells — stay in a destructive state, and that ongoing inflammation creates a hostile environment that blocks oligodendrocytes from laying down new myelin.

The fire never fully goes out. And repair can't happen on a live fire site.

This is the part of MS pathology that sits entirely outside what DMTs are engineered to reach. The immune suppression is upstream. The chronic neuroinflammatory environment that blocks remyelination is a separate problem — and it needs a separate solution.
